What Makes an Electronic Device Supplier ITAR-Compliant

ITAR (the International Traffic in Arms Regulations) governs the export and re-export of defence articles, defence services and related technical data on the United States Munitions List. For electronic device procurement, ITAR applies to: ruggedised communications hardware designed for military use, devices integrated into defence systems, devices containing controlled cryptographic modules, and certain night-vision, sensor and electro-optic equipment. The DDTC at the U.S. State Department publishes the regulations and registration requirements.

An ITAR-compliant electronic device supplier must, at minimum, be registered with the Directorate of Defense Trade Controls (DDTC) at the State Department, hold an active registration number, maintain compliance procedures for handling controlled items, and document chain of custody from manufacturer to end-use party. EAR (Export Administration Regulations, governed by the Department of Commerce) covers dual-use items and applies to a broader set of electronics including most consumer-class hardware exported internationally.

What Defence Procurement Teams Should Require

1. DDTC registration

The supplier must be registered with the State Department's DDTC and able to produce the registration number. Brokers handling defence articles between U.S. and foreign parties must also be DDTC-registered as brokers (separate from manufacturer/exporter registration).

2. Compliance officer or programme

Suppliers handling ITAR items should have a designated empowered official or compliance officer responsible for export control decisions. For programmes spanning multiple countries, a documented compliance programme covering jurisdictional review, end-use screening, and recordkeeping is essential.

3. Chain-of-custody documentation

ITAR shipments require documented chain of custody from manufacture (or import) through every intermediate party to the end-use party. The documentation includes serial-number tracking, transfer manifests, and end-use certification. For DSP-83 controlled items, the end-user signs a Non-Transfer and Use Certification before shipment.

4. End-use and end-user screening

Suppliers must screen the end-user and end-use against the State Department's denied-parties lists, sanctions lists, and prohibited end-use criteria. Documented screening with results is part of the export file.

5. Recordkeeping (5-year minimum)

ITAR records must be retained for 5 years from the date of the regulated activity. Suppliers should be able to produce records on request for any past shipment within that window.

EAR-Controlled Devices: A Larger Population

Most consumer-class electronic devices (smartphones, laptops, IoT cameras) are EAR-controlled rather than ITAR-controlled. EAR applies broader, lower-stakes export controls covering: encryption strength (a smartphone with strong encryption falls under ECCN 5A992 or 5A002 depending on configuration), end-user country (sanctioned countries are blocked), and end-use (military end-use to certain countries triggers heightened review).

For institutional buyers procuring devices for international deployment, EAR is more frequently the governing regime. Suppliers must screen end-users against the Entity List, the Denied Persons List and the Specially Designated Nationals list before shipment. Most institutional engagements are unaffected, but unscreened shipments to high-risk jurisdictions can trigger civil and criminal liability.

Common Compliance Failures and How to Avoid Them

  • Misclassification: assuming a consumer device is uncontrolled when its encryption configuration triggers ECCN 5A002. Always run jurisdictional review before shipment.
  • Inadequate end-user screening: relying on the end-user's representations rather than running independent denied-parties screening. Screen at order, screen at shipment.
  • Missing chain-of-custody documentation: failing to document intermediate parties or transfers. Each intermediate party must be on the export file.
  • Re-export without authorisation: exporting from one foreign country to another without re-export authority. ITAR re-export rules apply to defence articles regardless of geographic location.
  • Recordkeeping gaps: destroying records before the 5-year minimum. Keep all export records, including denied screenings, for at least 5 years.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Solaris Wireless registered with the DDTC?

Solaris Wireless coordinates ITAR-controlled deployments through DDTC-registered partners and direct registration where the engagement requires. Documentation of registration status is provided to qualified institutional buyers under NDA as part of due diligence.

Does Solaris handle EAR-controlled deployments?

Yes. Solaris handles EAR-controlled electronic device deployments globally including end-user screening, jurisdictional classification, and export-licence application support where required.

What is the difference between ITAR and EAR?

ITAR (State Department) governs defence articles on the U.S. Munitions List. EAR (Commerce Department) governs dual-use items on the Commerce Control List. Most consumer-class electronics fall under EAR; specialist defence hardware falls under ITAR.

Can devices be deployed to allied nations under ITAR?

Yes, with appropriate licensing. Many allied-nation deployments operate under ITAR exemptions or specific export licences. Solaris Wireless has supported allied-nation programmes including pre-cleared inventory and 72-hour rapid response for ruggedised communications hardware.

How long does ITAR/EAR compliance review take?

Standard EAR jurisdictional review and end-user screening completes in 1-3 business days. ITAR review and licence application can take 2-12 weeks depending on the article, the destination, and whether a licence is required.
