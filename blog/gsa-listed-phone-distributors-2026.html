How Federal Phone Procurement Works in 2026

Federal civilian and defence agencies procure mobile phones primarily through three contract vehicles: GSA Multiple Award Schedule (formerly Schedule 70, the IT-category schedule), NASPO ValuePoint state-level cooperative contracts (used by federal agencies for some categories), and direct-to-agency contract awards (typically through SEWP, NITAAC CIO-CS, or agency-specific procurement). The GSA itself publishes the Schedule contract listings.

Each vehicle has different qualification requirements, pricing models and ordering workflows. The right path depends on the agency's contracting officer's preferences and the deployment urgency. This guide walks through what federal procurement teams need to know about each vehicle and how to identify qualified distributors.

GSA Multiple Award Schedule: The Default Path

GSA MAS contract holders are the default supply path for most federal mobile device procurement. To list on GSA MAS, a vendor must complete a multi-month application covering financial capacity, past performance, pricing structure, and TAA compliance. The contract is awarded for 5 years with three 5-year option periods (potential 20-year tenure).

Federal buyers can search GSA Advantage and eBuy to identify schedule holders for specific device categories. For Android and iOS smartphones, the relevant SINs (Special Item Numbers) include 33411 (Telecommunications) and various IT hardware classifications under MAS.

TAA compliance is mandatory

The Trade Agreements Act requires federal devices to be sourced from designated countries. Most major OEMs (Apple, Samsung, Motorola/Lenovo) manufacture in TAA-designated countries (Vietnam, Thailand, India, Mexico, Korea), making TAA compliance straightforward. The vendor must produce country-of-origin documentation with each shipment.

NDAA Section 889 considerations

NDAA Section 889 (2019) prohibits federal agencies from procuring telecommunications equipment from Huawei, ZTE, Hikvision, Dahua, Hytera and certain affiliates. For phone procurement specifically, this affects baseband and modem chipsets in some devices and rules out a small set of low-end Android handsets that use prohibited components. Most major Android handsets are NDAA-compliant; verify with the vendor on the specific SKU.

NASPO ValuePoint and Cooperative Contracts

NASPO ValuePoint is a cooperative contract programme that allows state and local governments (and some federal agencies) to procure through pre-negotiated contracts. For mobile devices, NASPO contracts often offer aggressive pricing because the contract spans many state buyers.

Federal agencies that use NASPO must verify their authority to do so under their contracting officer's discretion. The advantage is faster procurement; the trade-off is less flexibility on configuration.

Direct-to-Agency Contracts

For larger or more specialised deployments, agencies issue direct-to-agency contracts through SEWP (NASA SEWP V), NITAAC CIO-CS, or agency-specific procurement. These vehicles support full RFP/RFQ workflows and accommodate custom requirements that GSA Schedule pricing does not.

Solaris Wireless has supported direct-to-agency engagements with U.S. Government agencies and allied nations for ruggedised communications hardware deployment. The 72-hour rapid response capability for pre-cleared inventory is unusual in the GSA Schedule supplier base and typically requires direct contracting.

Identifying GSA-Listed Phone Distributors

Procurement teams looking for a GSA-listed phone distributor should:

  • Search GSA Advantage and GSA eBuy by Special Item Number for the device category
  • Review past performance ratings in CPARS for shortlisted vendors
  • Verify the vendor's SAM.gov registration is active and the Cage Code matches the contract
  • Request a capabilities statement with past federal performance and OEM relationships
  • For specialist requirements (custom OS, ruggedised hardware, EOL components), engage vendors with documented federal experience even if their GSA Schedule scope is narrower

What Federal Procurement Teams Should Require

  • TAA-compliant origin documentation with each shipment
  • FIPS 140-2 or 140-3 validated encryption modules on devices that handle CUI or CNSS data
  • CAC/PIV reader configuration against the agency PKI
  • NDAA Section 889 compliance attestation for the specific device SKU
  • Chain-of-custody documentation from manufacturer to end user
  • End-of-life data destruction certificates for retiring devices
  • SAM.gov registration confirmation and active CAGE code

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I find a GSA-listed phone distributor?

Search GSA Advantage (gsaadvantage.gov) and GSA eBuy by Special Item Number for the device category. For mobile devices, relevant SINs include 33411 (Telecommunications) and various IT hardware classifications under the Multiple Award Schedule.

Are all phones sold to federal agencies TAA-compliant?

Yes, by law. Federal procurement requires TAA-compliant origin documentation. Most major Android and iOS handsets are manufactured in TAA-designated countries (Vietnam, Thailand, India, Mexico, Korea) and qualify.

What is NDAA Section 889 and how does it affect phone procurement?

NDAA Section 889 prohibits federal agencies from procuring telecommunications equipment from Huawei, ZTE, Hikvision, Dahua, Hytera and certain affiliates. For phone procurement, this rules out a small set of low-end Android devices that use prohibited components. Most major handsets are compliant.

What is the typical lead time for federal phone procurement?

Standard GSA Schedule orders typically deliver in 4-6 weeks. Direct-to-agency rapid-deployment can deliver in 72 hours from pre-cleared inventory for qualified emergency response engagements.
